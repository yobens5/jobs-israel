Israel's exposure is higher than comparable economies. Israel's large high-tech sector (software developers, data scientists, cybersecurity) and significant professional services workforce (lawyers, accountants, financial analysts) push the job-weighted average AI exposure to 4.9 — notably higher than Canada (3.7) and the US average — reflecting the concentration of knowledge-work occupations in Israel's labor market.

Occupations

The 112 occupations are drawn from Israel's Labour Force Survey and classified according to ISCO-08 (International Standard Classification of Occupations, 2008 revision), which Israel adopted as its national occupation classification standard. Occupations are organized into 14 sector categories reflecting Israel's labor market structure.

All 112 occupations have employment figures from the 2023 CBS Labour Force Survey, representing approximately 3.0 million jobs — a representative cross-section of Israel's ~4.3 million employed workforce.

Wage estimates

Salaries are reported in New Israeli Shekel (NIS / ₪) as annual figures, sourced from the CBS Israel Labour Force Survey 2022–2023 and the National Wage Survey published by the Israeli Ministry of Economy and Industry.

Currency context: As of 2024, 1 USD ≈ 3.7 NIS. Israel's minimum wage is ₪5,571/month (≈ ₪67,000/year). The national median wage is approximately ₪12,000/month (≈ ₪144,000/year). High-tech professionals, particularly in software and cybersecurity, often earn ₪300,000–₪500,000+/year, significantly exceeding the national median.

Wages are assigned at the occupation level based on CBS median wage tables. Occupations in the same ISCO sub-major group share base wage assumptions where individual data is unavailable. This is an approximation; actual wages within occupational groups vary considerably.

AI exposure scoring

Each occupation receives a score from 0 to 10 measuring how much AI is expected to reshape that occupation — both through direct automation and through productivity amplification (fewer workers needed per unit of output).

Scores are generated by GPT-4o-mini (OpenAI) reading a structured description of each occupation, including: ISCO-08 code, major category, employment 2023, projected growth, typical tasks, and Israel-specific labor market context. The model responds with a JSON object: {"exposure": N, "rationale": "..."}. Temperature is set to 0.2 for consistency.

Pipeline

Three scripts build the Israeli dataset from source data to the website:

ScriptOutputDescription
occupations_il.csvSource data112 ISCO-08 occupations with CBS Israel 2023 employment, wages, and labour market outlook
scores_il.jsonAI scoresGPT-4o-mini exposure scores (0–10) and rationale for each Israeli occupation

Known limitations

  • Representative sample, not complete census. The 112 occupations represent the major sectors of Israel's labor market but do not cover all ~500+ ISCO unit groups. Small and niche occupations are omitted.
  • Wages are approximated. Median wages are estimated from CBS Israel Labor Force Surveys and may not reflect the latest wage movements, particularly in high-tech where salaries shift rapidly.
  • National outlook is a proxy. Outlook ratings represent overall Israeli national trends and do not reflect significant regional variation (Tel Aviv high-tech vs. periphery regions).
  • AI exposure is an LLM opinion. Scores come from a language model reading occupation descriptions — they are not empirically measured automation probabilities. Individual scores should be interpreted as rough tiers, not precise values.
  • Defense and intelligence sector excluded. A significant portion of Israel's skilled workforce operates in classified defense, intelligence, and military technology sectors that are not captured in public CBS employment statistics.
  • Employment year is 2023. The dataset uses 2023 CBS data as the base employment year; rapid high-tech hiring or layoffs in 2024 are not reflected.
